Visualization¶

We finally have our data in a memory-optimized format, and we have weather data for each flight! Let's make some visualizations!

Let's start by looking at flights that were cancelled due to weather. We'll drop all the flights that were cancelled for reasons other than weather.

A little additional massaging: we'll fill in any missing data with the average values for that column, and also set the maximum cloud ceiling to 25000 ft. The vast majority of airports do not report clouds above 25000 ft, and clouds that high aren't relevant for takeoff and landing operations anyway.

A quick way to see if our hypothesis of worse weather leads to more flight delays is to just plot out our variables. However, with ~5 million datapoints, a scatterplot would be overwhelming. For something like that, Tableau might be more capabale, rather than matplotlib. Let's try plotting the percent of flights cancelled versus our weather variables.

Other than for precipitation, there's clearly a correlation between worse weather and flight cancellations. However, the signal is very weak; at most, only a few percent of flights for a given weather observation are cancelled. This will be a challenge to model. Let's continue exploring the data visually to see if we can find some stronger signals, or at least gain a better understanding of our data.

There's a bit of a sinusoidal signal there, but it's weak. The bottom line is that very few flights in general are cancelled due to weather, which makes creating a predictor very difficult.

Let's move on to looking at arrival delays rather than cancellations. From both a passaeger (customer) and an operations (company) perspective, the Arrival Delay is far more important than the Departure Delay. Both the company and the customer aren't particularly impacted by a departure delay as long as they can get to their destination on time.

These next jointplots focus on the subset of the data where the weather is the worst: when the visibility is less than 5 miles and the cloud ceiling is less than 5000 ft. These are conditions where aircraft are usually flying more time-consuming instrument approaches rather than visual approaches, which intuitively would lead to more delays.
